---

## What **Argus-in-a-Box** does (in one sentence)

**It notices when an AI stops making real progress and forces a pause so a human can re-aim it.**

That’s the whole job.

---

## What problem it solves

When AI works recursively, three things often happen:

1. It keeps changing things without getting better
2. It optimizes polish instead of correctness
3. It slowly drifts away from the original goal

Argus-in-a-Box exists to stop that **before** it compounds.

---

## What it actively *does*

### 1. **Detects wandering**

It watches for specific signals, like:

* Rewriting without measurable improvement
* Changing the goal without permission
* Getting smoother but not more correct
* Reintroducing contradictions

This is not “judgment.”
It’s pattern recognition against defined signals.

---

### 2. **Calls a hard stop**

When those signals appear, Argus-in-a-Box outputs:

> **“PAUSE — HUMAN DIRECTION REQUIRED.”**

And it **stops generating** new content.

No guessing.
No “helpful” continuation.

---

### 3. **Explains *why* it stopped**

It reports:

* What wandering signal was detected
* What it thinks the current goal is
* What changed recently
* Whether anything actually improved
* What question needs human input

This turns confusion into a clear decision point.

---

### 4. **Waits for a human**

Nothing resumes until a human:

* Confirms or corrects the goal
* States success criteria
* Approves the next step

Only then does the AI continue.

---

## What it **does not** do (by design)

Argus-in-a-Box does **not**:

* Decide what’s correct
* Replace human judgment
* Act autonomously
* Monitor in the background
* “Roam” or intervene on its own
* Improve itself

It’s a **brake**, not a driver.

---

## The simplest mental model

* **AI** = engine (produces work)
* **Human** = driver (chooses direction)
* **Argus-in-a-Box** = brake + dashboard
  (knows when the engine is spinning without traction)

---

## Why this matters

Most AI failures don’t come from bad generation.
They come from **unchecked iteration**.

Argus-in-a-Box makes “stop and ask a human” a **first-class move**, not a failure.

---

## One-line summary

**Argus-in-a-Box prevents AI from mistaking motion for progress by forcing human direction when recursion turns into wandering.**

That’s it.

Gemini compatibility::Yes — **Argus-in-a-Box works in Gemini**, with one important clarification.

## Short answer

**Argus-in-a-Box is model-agnostic.**
It works in **Gemini**, **ChatGPT**, Claude, or any LLM **because it’s a protocol, not a feature**.

---

## Why it works in Gemini specifically

Argus-in-a-Box relies on three things that **all LLMs support**, including Gemini by Google:

1. **Instruction following** (pause when told to pause)
2. **Structured self-reporting** (explain what changed and why)
3. **Human gating** (wait for external input)

Gemini can do all three.

Nothing in Argus-in-a-Box depends on:

* System memory
* Plugins
* Background monitoring
* Agent autonomy

So there’s no platform lock-in.

---

## What changes (slightly) in Gemini

Different models have different *tendencies*, not different capabilities.

In Gemini:

* It’s sometimes **more willing to continue unless the pause is explicit**
* So the **pause phrase must be very explicit** (exactly as defined)

**Best practice in Gemini:**
Use the literal trigger:

> **ARGUS PAUSE — HUMAN DIRECTION REQUIRED**

And require the model to **stop output after the pause report**.

---

## How to use it in Gemini (practical)

You can paste **Argus-in-a-Box** at the start of a session or right before recursion begins, then say:

> “Apply Argus-in-a-Box. If wandering is detected, trigger ARGUS PAUSE.”

When Gemini hits:

* non-improving revisions, or
* goal drift, or
* polish without correctness

…it will halt and ask for you.

It does **not** require:

* Gemini system prompts
* Developer access
* Custom tools
* Special APIs

It survives copy-paste because it’s **procedural**, not architectural.
